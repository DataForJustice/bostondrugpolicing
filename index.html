Politicians and law enforcement officials across Massachusetts now admit “we can’t arrest our way out of” drug problems.

Officials from both parties say that drug addiction is a public health—not criminal justice—problem.

But despite all of this agreement, Massachusetts still invests far more money in policing, jails, prisons, courts, probation, and parole than we do in drug treatment.

For example, here are the Governor’s proposed 2018 budget allocations for substance abuse services compared to the state police budget.

We spend so much money on criminalization even though it isn’t effective or smart financial policy. It costs $53k to keep someone locked up for a year, while only $5k to give someone a year of methadone treatment.

Just one arrest can permanently change one’s life for the worse, causing barriers to employment, schooling, and housing.

And a study from 2016 found that people are fifty times more likely to overdose just after they’re released from jail. (citation)

Unfortunately, our public policy does not reflect any of these facts. Instead of helping people, we are caging them.

“We can’t arrest our way out of this” rhetoric is nice, but it doesn’t mean much if it’s not backed up by action.

Let’s put our money where our mouth is and shift resources away from police and prisons, and towards public health.
